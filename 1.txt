
A Multi-Modal Disaster Risk Intelligence System
Natural and human-induced disasters (floods, earthquakes, wildfires, hurricanes, etc.) are increasing in frequency and severity. Modern technology produces vast multi-modal data during crises (text from tweets, images from satellites, sensor readings, etc.)
. However, emergency responders typically rely on text-based reports, leaving much information unexploited
. We propose an AI-driven system that ingests diverse data, applies machine learning to each modality, and fuses the results into actionable risk intelligence. By combining, for example, satellite imagery, ground sensor feeds, historical records and social-media sentiment, the system delivers a holistic, real-time view of evolving disasters
. This multi-modal approach builds on recent research: deep learning can extract features from raw images or text without handcrafted features
, and ensemble models (Random Forest, LSTM, CNN) achieve high accuracy in hazard prediction
. We leverage these advances to design a prototype system with separate modules for each disaster type, coordinated through a central “copilot” architecture
.

System Architecture and Data Pipeline
Our architecture (Figure 1) is modular and scalable. It begins with data ingestion of multiple streams:

Social Media: Twitter/Facebook posts, SMS messages and news feeds provide text (and sometimes images) from affected communities
. These act as “human sensors” offering rapid situational updates.
Sensors and Telemetry: Weather stations, river gauges, air-quality monitors, seismographs, etc., supply continuous numeric time-series data (e.g. rainfall, river levels, ground acceleration).
Remote Sensing: Satellite and aerial imagery (optical and infrared) capture on-the-ground conditions – flood extents, fire hotspots, storm clouds.
Historical Databases: Past disaster archives, digital elevation models, building inventories and infrastructure maps give context for risk modeling.
This raw data is fed through an ETL pipeline that cleans and standardizes each source and loads it into a unified database
. In practice, we follow the example of existing disaster-ML pipelines: our process_data.py script merges message and category CSVs, cleans text, and stores them in a SQLite database
. Similarly, we will preprocess sensor logs (handling missing data, smoothing) and geospatial imagery (cropping, indexing) during ingestion.

Once data is centralized, we implement two parallel ML pipelines: a Text Classification Module and a set of Hazard Forecasting Modules. The text pipeline reads crisis messages from the database, splits into training/test sets, and builds a natural-language processing (NLP) + machine learning model
. For our prototype, we use a multi-output Random Forest with tokenization and TF-IDF features to label each message with categories like “water needed” or “infrastructure damage”
. This mimics the “Disaster Response” pipeline used in Udacity projects, where classifying millions of emergency texts helps relief agencies prioritize resources
.

The forecasting pipeline contains hazard-specific models. Each model ingests the relevant streams (e.g. rainfall + river data + topography for floods) and predicts a risk metric. We anticipate using a mix of techniques: convolutional neural networks (CNNs) on imagery for spatial pattern recognition, recurrent networks (LSTM) on time-series sensor data, and ensemble methods (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) for tabular features. For example, recent studies show ML models (CNN, RF, SVM) can classify flooded vs. non-flooded areas from satellite images with >90% accuracy
. Likewise, combining weather forecasts with terrain data via ML outperforms some physics-only models. We will train and validate each model on historical and simulated disaster data, tuning hyperparameters via cross-validation. The ultimate outputs are severity scores or probability fields (e.g. flood depth, fire intensity) for each hazard.

An integrated fusion layer then combines all modality-specific predictions. Following the approach of Nayab‑Gauhar et al., our “data fusion algorithm” takes satellite-based estimates, sensor-derived metrics and social sentiment together
. This multi-modal fusion leverages complementary information – e.g. a sensor drought warning plus social-media reports of smoke might jointly raise wildfire risk
. Early fusion (merging raw inputs) and late fusion (ensemble of model outputs) techniques from the literature will guide design. As research suggests, fusing heterogeneous data yields more robust inferences than any single source
. We will also incorporate data validation (to filter noise or misinformation) as per the “3M” framework: multi-modal fusion, multi-source validation, and multi-agent collaboration
.

Finally, the system includes a Visualization & Alerting interface. We will build a Streamlit or Flask web app (similar to the referenced Disaster-AI-Predictor) that displays geospatial maps (using Folium) of current risk levels
. Plots and dashboards will summarize aggregated statistics (e.g. bar charts of incident categories, heatmaps of risk intensity). Word clouds can highlight frequent terms from text messages. When risk thresholds are exceeded, the system auto-sends warnings (via SMS, email or app notifications) to authorities and the public – a “customizable alert system” as described in [12].

Multi-Disaster Components
We design four primary subsystems (one per person) for specific hazards, each leveraging appropriate data and models:

Flood Prediction Module (Team Member A): Ingest precipitation forecasts, river/stream gauge data and terrain info. Use CNNs on radar or satellite imagery to map flood extents, and LSTMs on rain gauge series for lead-time forecasting. Recent reviews show ML (especially CNN/RF) outperform traditional hydrological models in flood mapping
. Outputs include flood inundation maps and risk indices. Alerts can be issued as river levels cross safety thresholds.

Earthquake/Tsunami Module (Team Member B): Ingest seismic waveform data (from networks like USGS) and crustal models. While long-term earthquake “prediction” is not yet possible, ML can improve rapid detection and magnitude estimation. We will implement an ML-based early-warning pipeline (similar to Transformer or CNN models on seismogram data) to quickly classify quakes and estimate their impact. If undersea quakes are detected, combine with ocean models to trigger tsunami scenario simulations. Social media monitoring can provide rapid situational reports (e.g. buildings shaken) even before official confirmation.

Wildfire Monitoring Module (Team Member C): Ingest satellite thermal/NDVI imagery, weather (temperature, humidity, wind) and forest maps. Train a model (e.g. CNN or U-Net) to identify active fire hotspots and predict spread based on fuel and wind conditions. Crucially, integrate social-media “human sensor” data: tweets or images about smoke or visible flames
. For instance, the “Sentimental Wildfires” model used Twitter sentiment plus historical fire data to improve accuracy
. We will apply sentiment analysis on geotagged posts and fuse that with physics-based fire spread models. The model will output hotspots and probability of fire growth; this multi-modal fusion is expected to detect fires faster than remote sensing alone
.

Cyclone/Hurricane Module (Team Member D): Ingest atmospheric data (pressure, sea surface temperature, humidity) from satellites and buoys. Implement an ensemble deep-learning storm model (e.g. stochastic neural network) to predict cyclone formation, track and intensity
. DeepMind’s Weather Lab has shown that AI can forecast hurricanes up to 15 days ahead, rivaling physics models
. We will adapt such architectures (e.g. CNNs on global weather grids, LSTMs on past track data) to produce a set of possible storm scenarios. Predicted storm surge extents could also be integrated, as per recent AI surge-forecasting research. Alerts will be geotagged to affected coastlines for evacuation planning.

Each team member will gather domain-specific datasets (e.g. NOAA archives, NASA imagery, local sensor feeds) and implement their module’s ML pipeline. All modules share the common database and fusion framework. We will coordinate to ensure consistent interfaces: e.g. all outputs provide latitude-longitude risk maps and a standardized “severity score”.

Implementation Plan
Data Integration: We will extend the ETL pipeline from the Disaster Response project
 to our diverse sources. Each member will write cleaning scripts for their data (e.g. merging .csv, handling missing values, normalizing units). All cleaned data goes into a shared SQL database, enabling cross-discipline queries.

Model Development: Each hazard team will experiment with ML architectures. We will use scikit-learn and TensorFlow (as in Nayab‑Gauhar’s repo
) for implementation. For example, team C might train a CNN on fire data while team D configures an LSTM ensemble for cyclones. Cross-validation on historical events will guide hyperparameter tuning.

Multi-Modal Fusion: After individual models produce predictions, a data-fusion module (possibly also ML-based) will combine them. For instance, a late-fusion stage could weight and average the normalized outputs. We will draw on the published data fusion algorithm which combines satellite imagery, ground sensors, and social media
. This unit will be jointly built by all members to ensure compatibility.

Interface & Deployment: As the system matures, we will merge modules into a single Flask/Streamlit app. The reference projects describe how to deploy a web app using the saved models
. We plan cloud deployment (e.g. on Heroku or AWS) so mentors and stakeholders can interact with the app remotely.

Team Roles: In summary, the four team members will focus as follows:

Member 1 (Floods): Flood data collection, remote sensing analysis, flood ML model.
Member 2 (Earthquakes): Seismic data pipeline, rapid detection ML, tsunami coupling.
Member 3 (Wildfires): Satellite/fire atlas analysis, social media mining, wildfire ML.
Member 4 (Cyclones): Meteorological data assimilation, AI weather model, storm surge. Collectively, all members will contribute to the ETL framework, multi-modal fusion and user interface.
Conclusion and Future Work
This proposed multi-modal system aims to enhance disaster preparedness by unifying heterogeneous data into intelligent forecasts and alerts. By leveraging advances in deep learning across text, imagery and time-series data
, we anticipate significantly faster detection and more accurate situational awareness than traditional methods. Recent AI-driven efforts support this vision: for example, integrating social-media and satellite data led to better wildfire predictions
, and Google DeepMind’s AI now forecasts hurricanes as well as physics models
. In line with cutting-edge research on “Disaster Copilots” and multi-agent systems, our architecture will provide a holistic, real-time operational picture by fusing all inputs
.

Future enhancements may include explainable AI modules (to increase trust), continual learning (to adapt as more data arrives), and extension to other hazards (e.g. pandemics or technological disasters). By assembling these components into a single platform, our team aims to deliver actionable disaster risk intelligence that empowers responders and communities well in advance of crises.
